Privacy, Ethics, and Data Sovereignty in Aotearoa Research

Why Privacy, Ethics, and Data Sovereignty Matter

  • Protects individuals and communities from misuse and harm in data practices.
  • Ethics ensures research is conducted with respect, fairness, and informed consent, safeguarding participants and relationships.
  • For MƒÅori, privacy extends beyond individuals to collective rights ‚Äî a foundation of MƒÅori Data Sovereignty (Kukutai et al. 2023).
  • Upholding these principles is both a legal duty under the Privacy Act 2020 and an ethical commitment to equity and responsibility in research (Chamberlain, Warner, and Penk 2022).
  • Builds trust, integrity, and accountability in research and institutions.

Privacy Legal Foundations in Aotearoa New Zealand

  • Privacy Act 2020 governs how personal information is collected, used, and shared (New Zealand Parliament 2020).
  • Replaced the 1993 Act to meet digital and data-driven realities.
  • Applies to all organisations ‚Äî public and private.
  • Overseen by the Office of the Privacy Commissioner.
  • Establishes 13 Information Privacy Principles (IPPs) guiding fair and lawful handling of personal data ‚Äî from collection to access.

Reflecting on the 13 IPPs

  • The 13 IPPs form the foundation of the Privacy Act 2020.

  • They are largely individual-centric ‚Äî focused on individual rights, not collective data control.

  • This raises key questions for MƒÅori contexts: Can privacy laws evolve to recognise collective data sovereignty? (Watts and Kukutai 2025)

MƒÅori Data Sovereignty ‚Äî What It Means

  • MƒÅori Data = information about MƒÅori people, communities, or resources
  • Includes:
    ‚Ä¢ Data generated by MƒÅori
    ‚Ä¢ Data about MƒÅori (collected externally)
    ‚Ä¢ Data about MƒÅori resources (land, language, environment)
  • Sovereignty = rights over ownership, governance, and use (Kukutai 2022).

Guiding Principles

  • Rangatiratanga ‚Üí authority over MƒÅori data
  • Kaitiakitanga ‚Üí guardianship & stewardship
  • Manaakitanga ‚Üí respect, reciprocity, consent

Implications for Research

  • Privacy = trust + equity

  • Balance privacy with public good outcomes

  • Health/social research ‚Üí ethics approval + MƒÅori consultation

  • Future Work

    • Future data systems should embed MƒÅori Data Sovereignty principles in their design and governance.
    • Emerging tools that enhance privacy (e.g. synthetic data, new data protocols) must still respect MƒÅori authority and values (Wang, Chukova, et al. 2024; Park and Shin 2021; Xu and Veeramachaneni 2021).

Research Data Landscape in New Zealand

Research Data Repositories in Aotearoa NZ

  • Students are encouraged to explore available open datasets:
    • data.govt.nz: NZ Government‚Äôs open data portal
    • Aotearoa Data Explorer: Stats NZ‚Äôs interactive tool for key social indicators
    • NZ Environmental Data (NIWA, MfE, Landcare Research)
    • Health Sector Data (publications and datasets)
    • Education Counts (education statistics)
  • These provide context, but our main focus will be the IDI (Statistics New Zealand 2025).

What is the IDI (and LBD)?

Launched in 2011, the IDI is a secure research database managed by Stats NZ that links multiple domains‚Äîincluding health, education, social services, justice, and migration‚Äîenabling longitudinal, population-wide analysis

Why the IDI is Important

  • Provides a holistic, integrated view of New Zealand society (Statistics New Zealand 2025).

  • Enables research into cross-sector issues:

    • Health inequalities (B. Milne et al. 2019)
    • Education ‚Üí employment transitions
    • Crime, justice, and social outcomes

  • Supports evidence-based policy and programme evaluation (Chamberlain, Warner, and Penk 2022).

  • Unique capability: population-level, longitudinal insights (B. J. Milne 2022).

  • Seen as a strategic national data infrastructure

Governance and Management

Access to the IDI is carefully controlled, following the Stats NZ ‚ÄúFive Safes‚Äù framework (Statistics New Zealand 2025):

  • Safe People ‚Äì Only trained, vetted, and approved researchers can access data.

  • Safe Projects ‚ÄìResearch proposals are assessed for legality, ethics, and public benefit.

  • Safe Data ‚Äì Only de-identified or securely linked data is provided.

  • Safe Settings ‚Äì Analysis occurs in secure, isolated Data Labs with strict technical and physical controls.

  • Safe Outputs ‚Äì Results are reviewed to prevent disclosure of identifiable information.

  • This framework ensures a balance between data utility and privacy protection, allowing safe, responsible use of IDI data.

Limitations of the IDI

  • Access constraints: analysis only in secure, on-site Data Labs, no offsite access (B. Milne et al. 2019).

  • Computational and software limitations: restricted processing power and limited programming languages (Wang, Chukova, et al. 2024).

  • Complex approval processes: time-consuming access and release checks due to confidentiality (B. J. Milne 2022).

  • Coverage gaps: little to no primary care, clinical notes, or private sector data.
  • Timeliness: lag of 1‚Äì2 years for some datasets.
  • Data quality: administrative datasets not collected for research purposes.
  • Access barriers: approval required; access restricted to secure labs.
  • Equity concerns: MƒÅori data sovereignty and representational issues.

Conclusion

  • NZ has many research data sources; students should explore open datasets independently (data.govt.nz 2025; Stats NZ 2025).
  • The IDI is the cornerstone of NZ‚Äôs integrated research data landscape.
  • Despite limitations, it remains a national treasure for research and policy.
  • Future efforts: make it more inclusive, timely, and accessible.

Emerging Data Science in Government: Synthetic Data and Explainable AI

Synthetic Data: Definition & Terminology

Context:
Synthetic tabular data generation learns the joint distribution of mixed continuous and discrete features and produces a synthetic table T_synth that retains the statistical properties of the original dataset. Modern generative AI models capture complex dependencies and enable realistic, high-utility synthetic datasets (Raghunathan 2021; Feuerriegel et al. 2024).

  • Artificially generated data mimicking real datasets (Raghunathan 2021).
  • Maintains statistical properties without exposing sensitive information.
  • Key concepts (Van Breugel, Qian, and Van Der Schaar 2023):
    • Fidelity: how closely synthetic data matches real data
    • Privacy-preserving: avoids revealing real individuals
    • Utility: usefulness for analytics and model training

Synthetic Data: Challenges & Motivation

Real data often limited in quantity, quality, and availability (Wang, Nguyen, and Simpson 2025).

Synthetic Data: Evolution of TDS Methods

  • Driven by data complexity, computing advances, and privacy needs.
  • Early phase: sampling methods to improve balance and efficiency.
  • Foundations: statistical models (imputation, probabilistic approaches).
  • Transition: shallow DGMs using MLPs ‚Üí start of neural synthesis.
  • Modern era: diffusion models and LLMs ‚Üí higher fidelity and flexibility.

Synthetic Data: Value & Applications

  • Safe data sharing across agencies and research sectors.
  • Addresses data scarcity, especially for small or minority groups.
  • Enhances model development, robustness, and system testing.
  • Supports policy evaluation and cross-sector innovation.
  • Health: simulate patient records for modelling and risk analysis.
  • Social policy: test interventions without exposing individuals.
  • Urban planning: generate census/traffic scenarios for planning.
  • Finance: create synthetic transactions for safe anomaly detection.

Explainable AI (XAI): Definition & Terminology

  • Techniques that provide insight into how ML models make decisions.
  • Key terms:
    • Transparency: model decisions are understandable
    • Interpretability: features driving predictions are explainable
    • Accountability: ensures ethical and responsible AI use

XAI: Challenges & Motivation

  • ML adoption requires both accuracy and interpretability.
  • Model Accurarcy vs. Interpretability trade-off.

XAI: Latest Methods & Models

  • Intrinsic Interpretability: simple models (e.g. LR, DT) (Ai and Narayanan. R 2021).
  • Post-hoc Explanations for complex models:
    • SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations): quantifies feature contributions.
    • LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations): local surrogate models for predictions.
    • Partial Dependence Plots (PDPs): Show how changes in one feature affect predictions while averaging over others.
  • Correlation vs. Causation: association <> causal impact: Counterfactual explanations: show what minimal changes alter predictions (Altman and Krzywinski 2015).
  • Model-specific interpretable architectures (e.g., attention-based models).

Explainable AI (XAI) in Government

XAI is essential in the public sector to ensure transparency, accountability, and equitable decision-making (Wang, Nguyen, et al. 2024).

Value

  • Builds trust and confidence among policymakers and the public.
  • Reduces black-box concerns and enables cross-agency ML adoption.
  • Strengthens responsible, transparent government decision-making.

Applications

  • Health: Explain risk predictions to support fair resource allocation.
  • Tax & Compliance: Make anomaly/fraud detection outputs interpretable for auditors.
  • Social Services: Reveal drivers of inequity to guide ethical, evidence-based interventions.
